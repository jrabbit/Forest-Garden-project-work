Anise Hyssop agastache foeniculum http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anise_Hyssop A vigorous generalist nectary plant, anise hyssop also makes a tasty tea. It can be used to flavor many foods including ice cream and muffins. It has a complex licorice flavor and has been used to lower fevers. You may see to see lots of pollinating animals patronizing the conical purple flowers, including butterflies and hummingbirds. (Wikipedia)
Mountain Mint pycnanthemum sp. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain_Mint While not a true mint, it is a member of the mint family, is edible and has menthol flavor. Unlike most mints, this plant does not spread by runners and instead stays clumped. (Wikipedia)
Lemon Balm melissa officinalis http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lemon_Balm Also in the mint family, the leaves of this plant can be used to flavor tea, candy and ice cream. It also can be used to repel mosquitos and in aromatherapy. Lemon balm should be avoided by anyone taking thyroid medication (thyroxine, for example), because it is thought to inhibit the absorption of the medication. This plant has been studied for its stress-reduction, antiviral and antibacterial properties. This species contains eugenol, which kills bacteria and has been known to calm muscles and numb tissues as well as terpenes that add to its soothing effects. (Wikipedia)
Blue Wild Indigo baptisia australis http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baptisia_australis This plant is a member of the pea family, and like other leguminous plants, it fixes nitrogen. It is considered poisonous although it is used medicinally for a number of ailments and was used by American Indians to treat toothaches and nausea. Cherokee Indians used this plant to make a blue dye, as did European settlers. If the stems are broken, a sap will be secreted that turns dark blue upon contact with the air. It produces deep blue, pea-like flowers. (Wikipedia)
White Clover trifolium repens http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_clover White clover is the primary groundcover in the garden. It is a generalist nectary plant, fixes nitrogen using symbiotic bacteria and has edible flowers that make a good salad addition. It seems to be low-growing enough to compete minimally with many plants.
Jerusalem Artichoke Helianthus tuberosus http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerusalem_Artichoke This Sunflower family plant is grown for its root vegetables that bear a resemblance to ginger root. It is said to have a potato-like consistency although more crisp. The tuber is often cooked by lightly steaming and has a mild, sweet and nutty flavor when prepared correctly. Jerusalem Artichoke was traditionally used by the Dakota, Shawnee and Miami American Indian tribes. The French explorer Samuel de Champlain found them being grown in Cape Cod in 1605. It was planted at Monticello and called by its French name, topinambours, by Thomas Jefferson. Helianthus tuberosa is often referred to as sunchoke. This plant’s high level of inulin*, a carbohydrate, makes it an important source of fructose for industry. “In the Baden-Wurttemburg state of Germany over ninety percent of the helianthus tuberosa grown is used to make a spirit called Topinambur, Topi or Rossler” (Wikipedia). Not digested well by some people.
Groundnut apios Americana http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apios_americana This native vine produces a root vegetable that is high in protein and has a flavor similar to potatoes. Improved varieties are being developed with larger, and less numerous tubers. Its vegetables can be found by digging up the cord-like roots and following them to find the swelled ‘beads’. Called “rosaries” by the early Canadian settlers, apios americana is another root crop that was used by American Indians.
Nodding Wild Onion Allium cernuum http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allium_cernuum This tasty and mild onion family native looks enough like a grass that it is sometimes weeded out by mistake.
Welsh Onion Allium fistulosum http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welsh_onion Though many people aren’t aware of it, many widely available scallions are allium fistulosum. The name Welsh onion is a misnomer in modern English and derives from the original Old English welisc or Old German welsche, meaning “foreign.” Both this crop and indigenous Britons were described as foreign, and as a result they share a name despite the fact that Welsh onion is Asian, likely originating from Siberia or China. It is known in French as ciboule and Portuguese as cebolinha and is an important ingredient in many Asian dishes.
Wild Leek Allium tricoccum http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wild_leek Wild leek, also called ramps or ramson is a much-cherished native onion. It’s edible greens and bulbs are delicious with eggs and can be used in place of onion or garlic. This forest-floor species is one of the first plants in the garden to emerge in spring, and is able to complete its entire seasonal leafy phase before the canopy trees leaf out.
Good King Henry Chenopodium bonus-henricus http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Good_King_Henry This species has also been called Lincolnshire spinach. Native to central and southern Europe and grown for hundreds of years as a vegetable in cottage gardens, this plant has been largely forgotten and is sometimes considered a weed. The shoots can be cropped back as they appear in spring and cooked like asparagus. In the summer the leaves can be harvested and cooked like spinach.
Giant Solomon's Seal polygonatum sp. In springtime, the shoots of this native plant can be cooked like asparagus. It has a graceful, arching form.
Paw Paw Asimina triloba http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asimina_triloba The large fruit of this handsome native tree has a custard-like consistency and delicious tropical flavor (something of a mix between banana, mango and coconut). Its seeds were likely dispersed by now-extinct North American megafauna (Barlow). Paw paw is the only member of the tropical custard-apple family that grows as far north as Michigan. It was cultivated by American Indians, as well as being an important food source for the Lewis and Clark expedition, and European settlers. Fans of asimina triloba have included Daniel Boone and Mark Twain. In modern times, powerful acetogenins in paw paw twigs have been recognized as a highly promising cancer-fighting compound.
Nanking Cherry Prunus tomentosa http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanking_cherry Originally from China, the small red fruit of this bush cherry is sweet and tart. In their first spring in the garden, the bushes bloomed impressively in late April.

Mayapple podophyllum peltatum no http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mayapple “The deeply cut, umbrella-like leaves of this common wildflower of woodlands grow to 12" across, and are carried on upright stalks 12 to 18" tall. This very tough plant spreads slowly to form a dense colony. It can make an excellent, handsome, long-lived, care-free groundcover, and is suitable for covering large areas. The leaves tend to go dormant in late summer, although dormancy is delayed if the plants do not lack for moisture. Mayapple thrives here in full sun, as long as the soil is moist, but it will tolerate drier soil and warmer climates better if it has some shade. The fruit, which looks like a small lemon and has a flavor reminiscent of tropical fruits, is edible when fully ripe in late summer. Fruit flavor is unusual, though agreeable, but the fruit is best eaten in small quantities. The fruit is a favorite food of box turtles and small rodents. Derivatives of Mayapple have shown promise in selectively killing cancer cells. All plant parts, except for ripe fruits, are poisonous if eaten. Exposure to sap can cause skin irritation in sensitive individuals.” (Tripple)

American Hazelnut corylus americana yes http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Hazelnut_(Corylus_americana) “This native species is the most adaptable and reliable nut-bearing plant in North America. Immune to filbert blight and bud mite. Resistant to frost in full flower, even when the temperatures drop into the low 20’s. Shrubs are tap rooted at first, eventually producing stolons that turn it into a multi-stemmed shrub from 4-12 ft. tall. This is the most dwarf species of hazelnut. Starting in their 2nd year, small runners come out 3-6 inches from the main trunk. Each of these sprouts will begin producing nuts in two years after the catkins are formed. The nutshell is thick, but the flavor is outstanding. The Native Americans drank a tea from the inner bark to cure hives and hay fever. The nuts were crushed and used as a body for soup or eaten raw with honey. Good for pollinating other hazels. Can produce in light shade. Hardiness -35 °F. ECOS: A strain with a larger nut and a slightly thinner shell. Good productivity. This is a combination of Winkler seedlings and other native Michigan selections. All are excellent nut producers. The quantity of nuts produced often bends the branches to the ground. Good pollinators too.” (Oikos)
